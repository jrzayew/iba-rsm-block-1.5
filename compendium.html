Quantitative Decision Making

RSM Erasmus University Rotterdam · Pieter van den Berg

00 Formulas at a glance

Profit per cell
\(\pi = \min(C,D)\cdot p - C\cdot c - \max(D{-}C,\,0)\cdot g\)
C=capacity · D=demand · p=price · c=cost/seat · g=goodwill
Expected Monetary Value
\(\text{EMV}(d) = \displaystyle\sum_{s} \pi(d,s)\cdot P(s)\)
Probability-weighted average profit. Choose max EMV.
Hurwicz criterion
\(H(d) = \alpha\cdot\max_s\pi + (1{-}\alpha)\cdot\min_s\pi\)
\(\alpha=1\) = MaxiMax · \(\alpha=0\) = MaxiMin
EVPI
\(\text{EVwPI} = \sum_s \max_d\,\pi(d,s)\cdot P(s)\)
\(\text{EVPI} = \text{EVwPI} - \max_d\text{EMV}(d) = \min_d\text{EOL}(d)\)

01 What is quantitative decision making?

Decisions are choices between alternatives evaluated by criteria. Intuition fails in complex situations — systematic methods compensate. Goal: better, more responsible decisions in a complex and dynamic world.

What makes decisions hard?
Many options · conflicting objectives · uncertainty about outcomes
What makes a good decision?
Evaluate both the outcome and the process — avoid outcome bias
Outcome bias
Judging decision quality solely by what happened, not by the reasoning used

02 Building the decision table

1
Decisions (strategies) — choices you control, e.g. 1 flight vs. 2 flights
2
Scenarios (states of nature) — future events you don't control, e.g. demand \(\in\{100,200,300,400\}\)
3
Decision table — profit \(\pi(d,s)\) for every decision × scenario combination

03 Three decision types

Type 1
Under Certainty
All outcomes known in advance. Just pick the best option.
Type 2
Under Uncertainty
Scenarios known; probabilities unknown. Use the five criteria.
Type 3
Under Risk
Probability distribution of scenarios is known. Use EMV / EOL.

04 Decision criteria under uncertainty

No probabilities available. Each criterion encodes a different attitude toward the unknown.

MaxiMax
Best best-case: take row max, pick the largest. Fully optimistic. → Airline: 400 (max=40)
MaxiMin
Best worst-case: take row min, pick the largest. Risk-averse. → Airline: 200 (worst=0 > −20)
Hurwicz (α)
Weighted blend: H = α·max + (1−α)·min. At α=0.8 → 200:16, 400:28 → choose 400
Laplace
All scenarios equally likely → simple row average. → 200 avg=9, 400 avg=10 → choose 400
MiniMax Regret
Build regret table, find row max-regret, pick the minimum. → 200:36, 400:20 → choose 400

Regret = col-max − cell · e.g. D=400: best=40, regret(200 seats) = 40−4 = 36

Exam trap: MaxiMax IS a special case of Hurwicz (α=1). Laplace is NOT — Hurwicz uses only best+worst; Laplace averages all values.

Lecture 2 · Week 2

Decisions Under Risk

RSM Erasmus University Rotterdam · Pieter van den Berg

00 Formulas at a glance

Expected Monetary Value
\(\text{EMV}(d) = \displaystyle\sum_{s} \pi(d,s)\cdot P(s)\)
Fold back from right. At squares, take max.
EVPI — perfect info
\(\text{EVwPI} = \sum_s \max_d\,\pi(d,s)\cdot P(s)\)
\(\text{EVPI} = \text{EVwPI} - \max_d\text{EMV}(d)\)
Bayes' theorem
\(P(S{\mid}P) = \dfrac{P(P{\mid}S)\cdot P(S)}{P(P{\mid}S)\cdot P(S)+P(P{\mid}W)\cdot P(W)}\)
EVSI — imperfect info
\(\text{EVwSI} = \sum_k P(\text{sig}_k)\cdot\max_d\text{EMV}(d{\mid}\text{sig}_k)\)
\(\text{EVSI} = \text{EVwSI} - \max_d\text{EMV}(d)\)
Efficiency of sample information
\(\text{Efficiency} = \dfrac{\text{EVSI}}{\text{EVPI}} \times 100\%\)
How close to perfect is the imperfect information?
Risk premium
\(\text{Risk premium} = \text{EMV} - X\)
Where \(U(X) = \text{EU}\). Find X on the utility curve.
Expected Utility
\(\text{EU} = \sum_s U(\pi(d,s))\cdot P(s)\)
Replace payoffs with utilities, then compute like EMV.
Joint & conditional probability
\(P(A\cap B) = P(A{\mid}B)\cdot P(B)\)
\(P(A{\mid}B) = \dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}\)

01 Decision trees

A graphical representation of the decision table — especially useful for sequential decisions.

■ Square
Decision node
You choose. Pick the branch with the highest EMV. Prune the rest.
● Circle
Event (chance) node
Nature chooses. Compute EMV = weighted average of branches. Probabilities must sum to 1.

Market entry example — decision table

Prior probabilities: P(Strong) = 0.45, P(Weak) = 0.55

Tree fold-back: how to solve

1
Start from the right (leaf nodes = raw payoffs)
2
At each circle: compute EMV = Σ (payoff × prob). Write it above the node.
3
At each square: take the maximum EMV branch. Strike through the rest.
4
Repeat leftward until you reach the root.

03 Conditional probabilities & Bayes' theorem

Research gives a Positive (P) or Negative (N) signal, but it's not 100% accurate. We know the reliability — then use Bayes to flip the conditioning.

Correct predictions
P(P|S) = 0.60 · P(N|W) = 0.70
Research says positive when market is strong, and negative when weak.
Wrong predictions
P(N|S) = 0.40 · P(P|W) = 0.30
False-negative: market strong but signal negative. False-positive: market weak but signal positive.

Step 1 — build the joint probability table

Each cell = P(signal|state) × P(state). Row sums give P(signal).

Strong (0.45)Weak (0.55)P(signal)
Positive (P)0.60×0.45 = 0.2700.30×0.55 = 0.1650.435
Negative (N)0.40×0.45 = 0.1800.70×0.55 = 0.3850.565
Col total0.450.551.00

Step 2 — compute posterior probabilities (divide cell ÷ row total)

Signal receivedP(Strong | signal)P(Weak | signal)

Base-rate fallacy: Even a 99% accurate scanner gives P(bomb|alarm) ≈ 0.5% when bombs are rare (0.01%). The prior base rate dominates. Click for the full calculation.

05 Utility functions & risk attitudes

EMV assumes the decision-maker is risk-neutral. In reality, people have different risk attitudes that can reverse the optimal decision.

Risk-avoider
Concave utility curve
Diminishing marginal utility of money. U(EMV) > EU. Needs > 50% to play a fair coin-flip game.
Risk-neutral
Linear utility curve
U(x) ∝ x. U(EMV) = EU. Happy to play any game with positive EMV.
Risk-seeker
Convex utility curve
Increasing marginal utility. U(EMV) < EU. Plays even unfair games for the thrill.
Risk-avoider — concave
Risk-neutral — linear
Risk-seeker — convex

Determining risk sensitivity — the indifference method

Set U(best) = 1 and U(worst) = 0. For intermediate value x, ask: "What probability p of winning (best) vs. 1−p of losing (worst) makes you indifferent to receiving x for certain?" → U(x) = p.

Worked example — risk-avoiding decision maker
U(100)=0.1, U(125)=0.5, U(150)=0.75, U(175)=0.85, U(200)=0.9
\(\text{EU} = 0.5\times U(100) + 0.5\times U(200) = 0.5\times0.1 + 0.5\times0.9 = \mathbf{0.50}\)
\(U(\text{EMV}) = U(150) = \mathbf{0.75} > 0.50 = \text{EU} \;\checkmark\;\text{risk-avoiding}\)
Risk-free value X: U(X)=0.50 → X=125. Risk premium = EMV − X = 150 − 125 = €25

OM · Lecture 1 · Week 1

Operations Management — Overview, Queueing & Learning

BT1214 · RSM Erasmus University Rotterdam

00 All formulas at a glance

Single-factor productivity
\(P = \dfrac{ ext{Units produced}}{ ext{Input used}}\)
One input only (labour-hrs, kg material, etc.)
Multi-factor productivity
\(P = \dfrac{ ext{Output}}{L + M + E + C + ext{misc}}\)
All inputs in the same unit (e.g. €)
Learning curve — arithmetic (doubling rule)
\(T_{2N} = L \cdot T_N\)
Only valid when quantity exactly doubles
Learning curve — logarithmic (any N)
\(T_N = T_1 \cdot N^{\,\log L / \log 2}\)
T1 = time for 1st unit · formula in exam appendix
M/M/1 — system time & length
\(W_s = \dfrac{1}{\mu-\lambda} \qquad L_s = \dfrac{\lambda}{\mu-\lambda}\)
M/M/1 — queue wait & length
\(W_q = \dfrac{\lambda}{\mu(\mu-\lambda)} \qquad L_q = \dfrac{\lambda^2}{\mu(\mu-\lambda)}\)
M/D/1 — queue wait & length (constant service)
\(W_q = \dfrac{\lambda}{2\mu(\mu-\lambda)} \qquad L_q = \dfrac{\lambda^2}{2\mu(\mu-\lambda)}\)
Utilization · idle prob · P(n > k)
\( ho=\dfrac{\lambda}{\mu} \quad P_0=1- ho \quad P_{n>k}= ho^{\,k+1}\)
Little’s law (always true)
\(L_s = \lambda W_s \qquad L_q = \lambda W_q\)
Queue ↔ system conversion
\(W_q = W_s - \dfrac{1}{\mu} \qquad L_q = L_s - \dfrac{\lambda}{\mu}\)

01 What is operations management?

Operations management (OM) creates value by transforming inputs into goods and services. Operations managers plan, organise, staff, lead and control this transformation.

3 essential functions
Marketing · Ops · Finance
Marketing generates demand. Operations creates the product. Finance/Accounting tracks performance and collects money.
Goods vs services spectrum
Most products are a mix
Automobile ≈75% good. Restaurant meal ≈50/50. Counselling ≈100% service.
Three course areas
Performance · Process · Planning
Area 1: Learning & Queuing. Area 2: Quality, Layout, Capacity. Area 3: Inventory, MRP, Scheduling.

02 Productivity

Productivity = output relative to input. Productivity growth is the main driver of rising living standards.

Single-factor example
\(\dfrac{1{,}000 ext{ burgers}}{500 ext{ hrs}} = \mathbf{2}\) burgers/hr
Multi-factor example
\(\dfrac{1{,}100}{(500 imes20)+1{,}000} = \dfrac{1{,}100}{11{,}000} = \mathbf{0.10}\) burgers/€
Labour €10 000 + equipment €1 000
Rate vs time: If service time = 20 sec → μ = 3600/20 = 180 per hour. Never plug the time in where the rate goes.

03 Learning curves (Module E)

Each time production doubles, unit time multiplies by the learning rate L. The curve flattens as volume grows.

Arithmetic
T2N = L · TN
Only at doubling points. L=0.60, T1=25 min → T2=15, T4=9, T8=5.4 min.
Logarithmic
TN = T1 · Nlog L / log 2
Works for any N. Rearrange to solve for L, T1, or N when one is unknown.

Exam example — shipyard

17th ship ≤ 65 days · T1 = 100 days · find minimum L
\(65 = 100\cdot 17^{\,\log L/\log 2} \;\Rightarrow\; \log L = \dfrac{\log(0.65)\cdot\log 2}{\log 17} \;\Rightarrow\; \mathbf{L = 0.90}\)

04 Queuing / waiting line theory

First studied by A.K. Erlang (1913). Core trade-off: cost of service capacity vs cost of waiting. Optimal = minimum total cost.

Terminology
Queue · Arrival · Channel · Phase
Queue = waiting line. Channel = # servers. Phase = # service steps. Discipline = order of service (usually FIFO).
Real examples
Bank · Harbour · Assembly
Bank: customers arrive, tellers serve, deposit is the process. Harbour: ships arrive, cranes serve, loading is the process.
Cost trade-off
Service cost ↑ → wait cost ↓
More servers = higher service cost but shorter queues. Neither extreme is optimal. Minimise total.

05 Probability distributions

Poisson
Arrival counts per interval
Mean = λ. Integer-valued. \(P(x)=e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x/x!\). Example: number of customers in 15 min.
Negative exponential
Service times (random)
Mean rate = μ. Mean time = 1/μ. \(f(t>x)=e^{-\mu x}\). Memoryless: past time gives no info about future.

06 Queuing models: M/M/1, M/D/1, M/M/S

1st letter
Arrival process. M = Poisson distributed.
2nd letter
Service process. M = neg. exponential (random) · D = deterministic (constant, exact).
3rd letter
Number of servers. 1 = single channel · S = multiple parallel channels.
ModelArrivalsServiceServersExample
M/M/1PoissonNeg. exp.1Info booth, coffee corner

07 5-step exam method & worked examples

1
Identify the model — M/M/1, M/D/1, or M/M/S?
2
Find λ and μ — same time unit. Service time = 20 sec → μ = 180/hr.
3
Identify performance measure — Wq, Ws, Lq, Ls, ρ, P0, or P(n>k)?
4
Select formula from appendix. M/M/S: use lookup table, then W = L/λ.
5
Plug in and compute.
Coffee corner — M/M/1 · λ=60/hr · μ=180/hr · find Ws
\(W_s = \dfrac{1}{180-60} = \dfrac{1}{120} ext{ hr} = \mathbf{30 ext{ sec total}}\)
Wq = 30 − 20 = 10 sec waiting. Lq = 60 × (10/3600) = 0.17 people.
Vending machine — M/D/1 · λ=144/hr · μ=240/hr · find Lq
\(L_q = \dfrac{144^2}{2\cdot240\cdot(240-144)} = \dfrac{20{,}736}{46{,}080} = \mathbf{0.45}\)
Answer B. On average 0.45 people waiting in queue.
